*Working Paper* — Revise & Resubmit: *Journal of Law, Economics, and Organization*

## Abstract

Learning from the experiences of other innovators is a crucial aspect of the innovation process when several workers or teams explore new research avenues in parallel. In such a setting, under-exploration may result as workers attempt to free-ride on the new ideas generated by co-workers. This paper studies optimal incentive schemes for innovation and shows that when workers can learn from each other's experience, incentives for innovation fundamentally differ from incentives for routine activities. Optimal incentives for routine activities take the form of standard pay-for-performance where only individual success determines compensation. In contrast, the optimal incentive scheme for parallel innovation tolerates early failure and provides workers with a combination of long-term individual and group incentives for joint success. This result is in line with the empirical regularity documented in previous work that team compensation, profit sharing, employee ownership, and stock option plans are positively correlated with innovative activity. I further show that there exists a *causal* link between such incentives and innovation. When subjects in a controlled laboratory experiment are asked to perform a task that requires creativity and exploration, they attempt to free-ride on the exploration of others if given standard pay-for-performance contracts. Innovation success and performance is highest when subjects receive a mix of individual and group incentives that reward long-term joint success.

## The Multi-Agent Bandit Problem

### Setup

The model uses a two-agent, two-armed bandit problem with one known arm to illustrate the tension between exploitation and exploration and the informational externalities that arise in parallel innovation settings.

There are two identical risk-neutral agents, $A$ and $B$, who live for two periods. In each period, the two agents simultaneously take an action $i$, each producing output $S$ ("success") with probability $p_i$ or output $F$ ("failure") with probability $1 - p_i$.

- **Action 1** is the conventional work method with known probability of success $p_1 = E(p_1) = E(p_1|S_1) = E(p_1|F_1)$.
- **Actions 2 and 3** are new work methods (exploratory), independently distributed with unknown probability of success satisfying $E(p_j) < p_1 < E(p_j|S_j)$ for $j = 2, 3$. New methods have initially lower expected success probability but, if successful, reveal a higher-quality method.

At the beginning of the second period, each agent observes the outcome of the other agent's first-period choice. This creates an informational externality: exploration of new work methods is a public good.

### Social Optimum

Three distinct action paths are possible:
- **Pure exploitation:** Both agents choose the conventional work method in both periods. Payoff: $4p_1$.
- **Single innovation:** One agent explores a new work method in period 1. If successful, both switch to the new method; otherwise both revert to conventional. Payoff: $p_1 + E(p_2) + 2p_1(1-E(p_2)) + 2E(p_2|S_2)E(p_2)$.
- **Parallel innovation:** Both agents explore different new work methods in period 1. Payoff: $2E(p_2) + 2E(p_2|S_2)[1-(1-E(p_2))^2] + 2p_1(1-E(p_2))^2$.

Pure exploitation is optimal when $p_1$ is sufficiently high; single innovation is optimal for intermediate values; and parallel innovation is optimal when $p_1$ is sufficiently low.

### Non-cooperative Interaction and Underexploration

**Proposition 1:** *There is inefficient underexploration* in the intermediate range where single innovation is socially optimal but pure exploitation is the equilibrium, and in the range where parallel innovation is socially optimal but single innovation is the equilibrium.

The pure exploitation equilibrium obtains when:
$$p_1 \geq E(p_2)\frac{1 + E(p_2|S_2)}{1 + E(p_2)}$$

The parallel innovation equilibrium requires:
$$p_1 \leq E(p_2)\frac{1 + (1-E(p_2))E(p_2|S_2)}{1 + (1-E(p_2))E(p_2)}$$

Comparing conditions from the social optimum and the Nash equilibrium immediately reveals inefficient underexploration. Agents free-ride on each other's exploration activities: exploration creates a positive informational externality which is not internalized in non-cooperative interaction.

## The Principal-Multi-Agent Problem

### Setup

Following Manso (2011), the model embeds the multi-agent decision problem into a principal-agent framework. A principal employs two agents to perform the tasks described above. Each agent incurs private costs: $c_1 \geq 0$ if choosing the conventional work method (action 1), $c_2 = c_3 \geq 0$ if choosing a new work method (actions 2 or 3), and $0$ if shirking (action 0). Shirking has lower success probability: $p_0 < E(p_i)$ for $i = 1, 2, 3$.

The principal publicly offers each agent $h = A, B$ a wage contract $\vec{w}^h = \{{\vec{w}_1^h, \vec{w}_{SS}^h, \vec{w}_{SF}^h, \vec{w}_{FS}^h, \vec{w}_{FF}^h}\}$ specifying wages in period 1 and period 2 contingent on the joint outcome history of both agents. The contract covers 20 wages for each agent in total.

An optimal contract implements a desired action plan at minimum expected wage costs, subject to incentive compatibility constraints $\mathrm{IC}_{(n_q^o p)}$:
$$W^h(\vec{w}^h, \langle i_l^j k_m \rangle) - C(\langle i_l^j k_m \rangle) \geq W^h(\vec{w}^h, \langle n_q^o p \rangle) - C(\langle n_q^o p \rangle) \quad \text{for } h = A, B.$$

Unless strictly cheaper for the principal to use delayed incentives or to condition wages on another agent's performance, the optimal contract pays agents earlier and solely rewards individual success.

### Optimal Incentives for Pure Exploitation

**Proposition 2 (Exploitation):** The optimal contract to implement exploitation for agent $A$ has first-period wages $w_{S.} = \alpha_1 + \frac{c_1(1+E(p_2))}{p_1 - E(p_2)}\left(\beta_1 - \frac{c_2}{c_1}\right)^+$ and $w_{F.} = 0$, and second-period wages $w_{.,S.} = w_{F.,S.} = \alpha_1$ and $w_{.,F.} = w_{F.,F.} = 0$, where $\alpha_1 = c_1/(p_1 - p_0)$.

Two key features: (1) incentives are entirely based on **individual performance** — each agent's wage does not depend on the other agent's performance; (2) incentives reward success in both periods with zero wages for failure. Because there is no learning across agents under pure exploitation, the performance of the other agent conveys no new information.

### Optimal Incentives for Parallel Innovation

The optimal incentive scheme for parallel innovation fundamentally differs from the exploitation contract:

1. **Long-term incentives:** Wages paid in the second period are higher than those in the first period — early failure is tolerated with non-trivial second-period wages even for agents who fail in the first period.

2. **Group incentives:** An agent's second-period wages depend on the outcome of the other agent's first-period choice. Specifically, the agent receives a bonus when the other agent is successful in the first period.

This combination directly addresses the free-riding problem: group incentives allow agents who explore and fail to benefit from the spillovers of the other agent's successful exploration, thereby internalizing the informational externality.

## Laboratory Experiment

### Design

The experiment uses a real-effort creative task building on Ederer and Manso (2013) in which subjects must explore a grid of letters to find a word worth more than a threshold point value. The task involves genuine exploration of unknown approaches (new words) versus exploitation of a known safe option (a conventional word). Subjects observe and learn from the actions of other subjects in their matching group.

**Treatments:**
- **Standard Pay-for-Performance (PFP):** Subjects rewarded solely for individual success from the beginning.
- **Long-Term Individual (LTI):** Incentives tolerate early failure and reward long-term individual success.
- **Long-Term Group (LTG):** Combines long-term individual and group incentives that reward joint success.

**Observational learning:** Subjects observe the action choices of other players, creating social learning as in the theoretical model.

### Key Results

1. **Social learning improves innovation:** When subjects can observe others' choices, innovation performance increases. The mutual exchange of information is beneficial for innovation.

2. **Standard PFP causes free-riding:** When subjects are given standard pay-for-performance incentives that do not tolerate early failure, they significantly reduce their own individual exploration activities and instead rely on the innovating efforts of others. This demonstrates the free-rider problem predicted by the theory.

3. **Group incentives are optimal:** Innovation success and performance is highest when subjects receive long-term group incentives (LTG) that reward joint success. This improvement is primarily driven by subjects increasing their individual exploration over and above the level elicited by contracts that only reward long-term individual performance.

4. **Causal link established:** The experiment establishes a causal link between group incentives and innovation, validating the positive correlations documented in empirical studies between team compensation, profit sharing, employee ownership, stock option plans, and innovative activity.

---

## Conclusion

This paper provides the first formal analysis of incentive design for innovation for several innovators working in parallel. The theoretical model shows that optimal incentives for parallel innovation — unlike incentives for routine tasks — must tolerate early failure and include group incentives for joint success. This structure internalizes the informational externality created when exploration results are public goods.

The laboratory experiment confirms a causal link between incentive structure and innovation: standard pay-for-performance contracts cause free-riding on colleagues' exploration, while long-term group incentives eliminate free-riding and maximize innovation performance. These findings are consistent with empirical evidence that innovative firms disproportionately use team compensation schemes, and provide a theoretical and experimental foundation for understanding why such schemes are effective specifically in innovation settings.
