# Understanding Mechanisms Underlying Peer Effects: Evidence from a Field Experiment on Financial Decisions

**Leonardo Bursztyn** (University of Chicago; NBER)
**Florian Ederer** (Boston University; CEPR, ECGI, and NBER)
**Bruno Ferman** (São Paulo School of Economics – FGV)
**Noam Yuchtman** (London School of Economics; NBER)

*Econometrica*, Vol. 82, No. 4, pp. 1273–1301, July 2014

---

## Abstract

We study social influence on financial decisions using a natural field experiment at a Brazilian retail bank. Using a novel experimental design, we separately identify two distinct channels through which peers affect economic decisions: (i) social learning — learning from a peer's revealed preference about the quality of a financial product — and (ii) social utility — utility derived directly from owning the same asset as a peer. Both channels independently and significantly increase take-up of the financial asset. We also find that financial sophistication moderates the social learning channel but not the social utility channel.

---

## Setting and Product

The experiment was conducted with clients of a Brazilian retail bank and brokerage. The financial product was a novel investment asset combining a long/short mutual fund with a LCI (Letras de Crédito Imobiliário — a tax-advantaged fixed-income instrument similar to a Certificate of Deposit). The product was introduced specifically for this study and was unfamiliar to all participants.

---

## Experimental Design

### Sample

The study recruited 150 pairs of socially connected clients — pairs who had previously identified each other as friends or family members and who both had brokerage accounts at the bank. Within each pair, one client was designated the "first mover" and the other the "second mover."

### Double Randomization

The design uses a double randomization to disentangle the two channels of peer effects:

**First randomization:** At the time the first mover is offered the asset, the offer is randomly assigned to:
- *Lottery treatment:* The first mover receives the asset, but via a lottery — so whether the first mover actually obtains the asset is determined by chance, independent of whether the first mover wanted it.
- *Non-lottery treatment:* The first mover receives the asset if and only if she chooses to take it up.

**Second randomization:** The second mover is randomly told (or not told) whether the first mover *wanted* the asset (i.e., whether the first mover signed up for the offer).

This double randomization creates four cells, three of which are used to identify the channels:

| Condition | What the second mover learns | Channel identified |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Baseline** | Nothing about the first mover | Baseline take-up |
| **2 — Social learning only** | First mover wanted the asset, but lottery blocked possession | Social learning only |
| **3 — Social learning + possession** | First mover wanted and actually obtained the asset | Both channels jointly |

Condition 3 minus Condition 2 isolates the **social utility** channel (possessing the same asset as a peer). Condition 2 minus Condition 1 isolates the **social learning** channel (learning that a peer revealed a preference for the asset).

---

## Results

### Take-Up Rates by Condition

| Condition | Second-Mover Take-Up Rate |
|---|---|
| 1 — No information (baseline) | 42% |
| 2 — Social learning only (peer wanted but lottery blocked) | 71% |
| 3 — Social learning + possession | 93% |

**Social learning effect:** Condition 2 − Condition 1 = **29 percentage points** (significant).

**Social utility effect:** Condition 3 − Condition 2 = **22 percentage points** (significant).

Both channels are individually large and statistically significant. Together they explain nearly the entire difference between baseline take-up (42%) and take-up when a peer obtains the asset (93%).

### Financial Sophistication and Social Learning

The social learning effect is moderated by the second mover's financial sophistication:

- **Less financially sophisticated investors** exhibit a larger social learning effect: they update their assessment of the asset quality more strongly when told that a financially sophisticated peer chose it.
- **More financially sophisticated investors** are less influenced by the social learning channel — they have better independent information with which to evaluate the asset.

Financial sophistication does **not** moderate the social utility channel — the direct pleasure of owning an asset one's peer owns does not depend on how financially literate the investor is.

### Robustness

The findings are robust to:
- Alternative definitions of financial sophistication (self-reported vs. portfolio-based measures)
- Controlling for observable characteristics of investors
- Placebo tests using assets not associated with peer information

---

## Conclusion

Social interactions affect financial decisions through two distinct, separable channels. Social learning — updating beliefs about product quality based on a peer's revealed preference — and social utility — a direct preference for consuming what one's peers consume — both independently drive take-up of a new financial product. The social learning channel is stronger among less financially sophisticated investors, consistent with a rational information-based mechanism. The social utility channel operates uniformly across sophistication levels, consistent with a preference-based mechanism such as conformism, status concern, or envy.

These findings have implications for the design of financial products, the spread of financial behaviors through networks, and the interpretation of observational evidence on peer effects in financial markets.
