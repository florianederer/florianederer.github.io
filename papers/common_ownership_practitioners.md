# Common Ownership: A Guide for Antitrust Practitioners

**Authors:** Florian Ederer (Boston University Questrom School of Business, CEPR, ECGI, and NBER), Isabel Tecu (Charles River Associates)

**Published in:** *Antitrust Economics for Lawyers*, Chapter 7, LexisNexis Antitrust Law & Strategy Series, 2025

**JEL Codes:** L41 (Monopolization; Horizontal Anticompetitive Practices), D43 (Oligopoly and Other Forms of Market Imperfection), K21 (Antitrust Law)

**Keywords:** Common Ownership, Market Power, Antitrust, Competition Policy

---

## Abstract

This chapter reviews recent theoretical and empirical research on the competitive effects of common ownership, the situation in which investors hold substantial stakes in multiple firms that compete in the same markets. We first describe how economists measure common ownership and document its sharp rise over the past several decades, particularly driven by institutional investors. We then survey the growing body of empirical work examining whether common ownership measurably softens competition. Studies across industries such as airlines, banking, pharmaceuticals, and venture capital find evidence that common ownership can raise prices, reduce entry, alter managerial incentives, and influence innovation, though results remain contested and depend on identification strategies and industry context. We also discuss mechanisms through which common ownership may affect firm behavior, such as voting, direct engagement, executive compensation, and board interlocks, as well as broader implications for labor markets, production networks, and corporate governance. The chapter concludes by outlining implications for antitrust enforcement, highlighting both the legal challenges posed by minority shareholdings and the evolving treatment of common ownership in merger review and policy debates.

## 1. Introduction

Common ownership refers to investors holding significant shares in multiple competing firms. Antitrust concerns arise to the extent common ownership softens competition among the commonly owned, competing firms (the "common ownership hypothesis"). Common ownership concerns do not require explicit coordination or collusion; instead, common ownership might align the incentives of competing firms in a way that softens competition, thereby leading to higher prices, lower levels of investment, less innovation, or other anticompetitive effects. Several mechanisms have been proposed through which this softening of competition may occur, including direct communication between investors and portfolio companies, shareholder voting, executive incentive schemes, and overlapping board members.

Antitrust enforcers and policymakers have been paying attention to common ownership concerns. Soon after the first empirical studies finding anticompetitive effects of common ownership were published, the U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) made common ownership the topic of one of their hearings, and the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) expressed interest in the topic. Likewise, the European Commission included analysis of the effects of common ownership in a merger decision (Dow/DuPont, 2017).

The U.S. Merger Guidelines introduced the term "common ownership" in their 2023 edition, and the February 2025 U.S. merger filing rules include provisions for the antitrust agencies to gather more information on minority investors on all mergers that trigger U.S. filing requirements. In 2024, several Republican-led U.S. states sued large institutional investors under both Clayton Act Section 7 and Sherman Act Section 1, as well as several state antitrust laws, alleging that these investors' holdings in, and resulting engagements with, competing coal companies were anticompetitive. In May 2025, the DOJ and the FTC filed a statement of interest in the case, explaining that while antitrust safe harbors for passive investment protect most index fund investing and beneficial corporate governance advocacy, they do not protect the use of commonly managed stock in competitors to encourage market-wide reductions in output.

The notion that shareholder common ownership can cause firms to soften competition goes back to at least the early 1980s. The driving assumption is that firms, instead of maximizing their own profits, maximize a weighted average of their shareholders' profits. Combined with a desire of shareholders to diversify their investments across multiple competing firms in a sector, the common ownership hypothesis suggests that firms will seek to maximize joint profits, and in the extreme, mimic an industry-wide monopoly.

Empirical evidence for the common ownership hypothesis is debated among economists and legal scholars. Some studies have found evidence consistent with the hypothesis in industries like airlines and banking, while other studies have criticized the methodology or found little to no effect on competition.

This chapter provides an overview of the current state of economic research on the common ownership hypothesis and its relevance for antitrust practitioners. After briefly explaining how economists measure common ownership (Section 2), we describe the increase of common ownership over the last decades (Section 3). Sections 4 through 6 review the recent academic research: Section 4 looks at empirical evidence for competitive effects in traditional horizontal product markets (price effects, entry effects, economy-wide effects); Section 5 summarizes the literature on specific mechanisms by which common ownership may adversely affect competition (voting, managerial incentives, director interlocks); Section 6 provides an overview of other aspects of firm behavior affected by common ownership (innovation, vertical relationships, labor markets). Finally, Section 7 examines implications for antitrust enforcement. Section 8 concludes.

## 2. Measurement of Common Ownership

The fundamental assumption of the common ownership hypothesis is that a firm, rather than maximizing its own profits, maximizes a weighted sum of its shareholders' profits including the interests its shareholders have in other firms. Specifically, a firm weighs each shareholder's profits proportional to the degree of control or influence that shareholder has over the firm (the "control share" or "control weight"). Each shareholder's profits are the sum of profits across the firms in which they have ownership stakes, weighted by the shareholder's financial interest in each (the "ownership share").

The ownership shares are simply the shares of a firm's profits accruing to each shareholder, generally determined by the fraction of outstanding shares owned. The extent of "control" is not as easily quantifiable. Most empirical studies assume control is proportional to ownership, adopting the "one share, one vote" assumption that characterizes most publicly traded U.S. firms.

To account for common ownership in estimates of market concentration, economists often use a modified version of the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) that incorporates ownership and control shares — the "modified HHI" (MHHI). The most widely adopted approach in the empirical literature has been to focus on **profit weights** between two firms, which measure the weight that one firm places on the profits of another due to common ownership (Backus, Conlon, and Sinkinson 2021). These profit weights are a function of the ownership and control shares, as detailed in the Technical Appendix.

## 3. Increase of Common Ownership in Recent Decades

Beginning in the early 1980s, various authors observed that shareholder diversification can, at least in theory, lead firms to internalize the externalities they impose on each other (Rubinstein 1983; Rotemberg 1984). Policy concerns did not arise at the time because of comparatively low levels of common ownership. That has changed.

Backus, Conlon, and Sinkinson (2021) study the ownership of all firms in the S&P 500 from 1980 through 2017. They find that the average pairwise profit weight implied by the common ownership hypothesis more than tripled among S&P 500 firms, from just over 0.2 in 1980 to almost 0.7 in 2017. A profit weight of zero corresponds to one firm placing no weight on its rival's profits; a profit weight of one corresponds to full internalization of rivals' profits, comparable to a merger.

Amel-Zadeh, Faasse, Li, and Starks (2022) merge SEC 13-F filings by institutional investors with 13-D and 13-G filings by blockholders and insider filings for 2003–2020. They find: (i) studying only institutional investors may over- or understate true common ownership because non-financial blockholders and insiders have less diversified portfolios while activists hold surprisingly diversified portfolios; (ii) common ownership in the same industry is generally higher than common ownership across industries, implying that policymakers could reduce same-industry common ownership without restricting index investing overall.

Li, Norden, and Spalt (2023) and Eldar and Garber (2024) document significant common ownership in venture capital portfolio holdings. Antón, Giné, Ederer, and Ramirez-Chiang (2025) extend the analysis to 49 countries and show that common ownership is rising globally. Common and universal ownership continues to rise across public firms and is driven by neither a single factor nor a single type of investor.

## 4. Empirical Evidence Pointing to Anticompetitive Effects of Common Ownership

### 4.1 Price Effects in Specific Industries

The first empirical study to investigate whether common ownership affects competition is Azar, Schmalz, and Tecu (2018) on the U.S. airline industry. They find that airline fares are higher on routes and in quarters in which common ownership is more pronounced. Using the MHHI to measure common ownership, they document that accounting for common ownership increases market concentration by about 2,000 HHI points on average across routes in 2014 — an order of magnitude larger than the threshold above which the 2010 Horizontal Merger Guidelines considered mergers presumptively anticompetitive (200 HHI point increase in markets with post-merger HHI above 2,500). To address endogeneity concerns, Azar, Schmalz, and Tecu (2018) use a merger between asset managers as a natural experiment that caused a sizable increase in common ownership unrelated to airline prices.

Other studies followed. Azar, Raina, and Schmalz (2021) apply a similar methodology to the U.S. banking industry and find that increased common ownership among banks is associated with lower deposit rates. Torshizi and Clapp (2019) document price effects in the seed sector; Liu and others (2024) find evidence that common ownership increases hospital prices.

The most fundamental methodological critique of these studies is that the MHHI is a function of market shares, and market shares are determined by prices, creating a feedback loop. Some studies suggest this endogeneity can entirely explain the results (Kennedy and others 2017; Dennis, Gerardi, and Schenone 2022). However, Park (2019) uses a structural model that avoids certain endogeneity concerns and finds results consistent with Azar, Schmalz, and Tecu (2018). Azar, Raina, and Schmalz (2022) structurally estimate common ownership effects in the airline industry and find evidence in favor of the common ownership hypothesis, though their estimates of the impact are substantially smaller than those implied by full internalization. Backus, Conlon, and Sinkinson (2021), in a structural study of ready-to-eat cereal, cannot reject that firms price in line with "partial" common ownership (placing some positive weight on rivals) though they reject full internalization.

### 4.2 Effects on Entry

In addition to changing how firms compete, common ownership can change the set of firms that compete. Newham, Seldeslachts, and Banal-Estanol (2025) analyze generic pharmaceutical entry into markets opened by the end of regulatory protection. They find that an increase in common ownership leads to a significant reduction in generic entry. Xie (2020) finds that institutional investors' common holdings between pharmaceutical generic and brand companies increase the likelihood of settlement agreements when a generic company disputes the brand's patent validity — another pathway through which entry can be deterred. Ruiz-Perez (2019) provides evidence that common ownership's main effect in airlines comes through entry decisions rather than price-setting given existing market structure.

### 4.3 Economy-wide Effects

Boller and Morton (2019) study abnormal stock price returns when common ownership among competing firms increases due to a new entrant in the S&P 500. They find that abnormal returns increase with the size of the increase in common ownership, suggesting that effects are not confined to specific industries.

Ederer and Pellegrino (2026) analyze the overlapping networks of product market competition and ownership among U.S. public firms in a general equilibrium model of oligopoly. Under the assumption that firms maximize a share-weighted average of their shareholders' income, they find that the welfare cost of common ownership, measured as the ratio of deadweight loss to total surplus, increased about ninefold between 1995 and 2021, and led to a significant reallocation of surplus from consumers to producers. Even under more realistic assumptions of corporate governance (e.g., only blockholders influence firm decisions, or frictions limit common shareholder influence), the estimated deadweight loss of common ownership ranges between 3.5% and 13.2% of total surplus in 2021.

## 5. Mechanisms of Common Ownership

A critical question raised by empirical studies is how shareholder interests translate into firm behavior. How could shareholders get firms to maximize a weighted sum of shareholder profits rather than the firm's own profits?

Shekita (2022) documents and taxonomizes thirty cases of common owner intervention, spanning a broad set of industries, and identifies that common owners use voting, direct engagement, and managerial compensation to influence firm decision-making.

### 5.1 Unilateral or Coordinated?

The common ownership hypothesis generally assumes firms act in their **unilateral** interest by maximizing a weighted sum of their shareholders' profits — a form of unilateral effects, not coordinated effects. The outcome resembles collusion (charging higher prices or producing lower quantities), but does not require firms to coordinate their competitive actions.

The theoretical effect of common ownership on the likelihood of collusion is in fact ambiguous: on the one hand, common ownership links lead firms to internalize some of the cost of deviating from a coordinated equilibrium, making collusion more stable; on the other hand, common ownership allows firms to charge higher prices in the competitive equilibrium, reducing the benefit of coordination and making punishment from deviation less severe, which would tend to make collusion less stable. The boundary between "unilateral" and "coordinated" effects is blurred in the common ownership context, since common ownership changes a firm's unilateral profit maximization problem in a way that can also be modeled as enabled coordination.

### 5.2 Voting and Direct Engagement

How and why institutional investors vote their shares is a topic of increased study. Shekita (2022) documents multiple cases in which large institutional investors voted in line with their interests in competitors. Institutional investors also publicly tout engagement efforts with portfolio firms. BlackRock engaged with over 2,500 unique companies in 2023. Shekita (2022) provides examples of "calls to action" by common owners, including calls to cut capacity on airlines' earnings calls and calls to set emission targets for oil and gas companies.

Antón, Azar, and Giné (2022) and Antón, Azar, Giné, and Pellegrino (2023) provide evidence that common ownership links may lead acquirers to propose "bad deals" because their shareholders benefit via ownership stakes in rivals, and that common owners vote in favor of mergers in ways that may suppress competition.

### 5.3 Managerial Incentives and Passivity

Passivity is a plausible mechanism through which common ownership produces anticompetitive effects. Common ownership can weaken management incentives to compete aggressively by replacing undiversified shareholders who favor aggressive competition with diversified shareholders with common ownership stakes who do not favor aggressive competition.

Antón, Ederer, Giné, and Schmalz (2023) explore this theoretically and empirically. Their model highlights how firms' hierarchical structures and limited investor transparency allow managerial incentives to shape competition, without requiring coordination or active intervention. Common owners passively approve compensation plans that offer high, performance-insensitive pay, making governance passivity an optimal strategy. Empirically, they find a strong negative relationship between common ownership and CEO wealth-performance sensitivity: a shift from the 25th to the 75th percentile of common ownership correlates with a 10.5% decline in CEO pay-performance sensitivity. They also show that common ownership may raise prices by reducing productivity and increasing costs, without necessarily increasing markups.

Forsbacka (2024) finds that proxy advisor ISS is more likely to support mergers, oppose performance-sensitive executive compensation, and endorse director interlocks at firms with higher levels of common ownership.

### 5.4 Director Interlocks

Shared board directors among competing firms ("director interlocks") represent another mechanism. While U.S. antitrust laws generally prohibit director interlocks among competitors under Section 8 of the Clayton Act, overlapping directors are relatively frequent in practice. Nili (2020) finds that in 2016, 16% of S&P 1500 companies shared at least one director with other companies in the same four-digit SIC code. Manjunath and others (2024) find that many disease indications in life sciences have dozens of director interlocks.

Director interlocks could translate anticompetitive incentives into firms' competitive strategies by facilitating information sharing. Azar and Völker (2022) and Eldar and Garber (2025) document that common ownership correlates positively with shared directors. Gopalan and others (2024) find that a firm's margins and prices increase after it forms a new director interlock with a competitor. Poberejsky (2024) finds that director interlocks with a competitor improve firm performance, lead firms to patent in more distant technologies (avoiding redundancy), and dampen competition — illustrating the trade-off between efficiency and reduced competition. Eldar and Garber (2024) find that venture capital common ownership primarily operates through the presence of shared directors, which serve as a key conduit for sharing information and facilitating an efficient allocation of resources.

## 6. Beyond Horizontal Product Market Effects of Common Ownership

### 6.1 Innovation

Firms have inefficiently low incentives to innovate in the presence of technological spillovers, when other firms benefit from their inventions and the innovating firm does not capture the full surplus. Common ownership mitigates this impediment to innovation. By contrast, without technological spillovers, innovation steals market share from rivals; in that case, more common ownership reduces innovation.

López and Vives (2019) show in a Cournot oligopoly with technological spillovers that increases in common ownership increase innovation and output, but only if spillovers are sufficiently large.

Antón, Ederer, Giné, and Schmalz (2025) extend this analysis to allow for common ownership across the entire economy, product differentiation, and common ownership varying across all firm pairs. They find that common ownership between closer product market competitors decreases innovation, but common ownership between closer technological neighbors increases innovation. The sign and magnitude of the relationship between common ownership and corporate innovation varies considerably across the universe of firms.

He and Huang (2017) examine whether common blockholders affect corporate innovation and find evidence suggesting common ownership improves innovation productivity. Kostovetsky and Manconi (2020) show that increases in shared institutional ownership arising from firm addition to an index are followed by more patent citations. Li, Norden, and Spalt (2023) find that common venture capital owners actively engage to hold back projects, withhold funding, and redirect innovation at lagging startups while improving overall innovation efficiency.

### 6.2 Vertical Relationships and Input-Output Networks

Several papers document effects of common ownership in vertical relationships (Geng, Hau, and Lai 2017; Kedia, Starks, and Wang 2017; Chen and others 2024). Vertical common ownership may have pro-competitive effects such as eliminating double marginalization, much as vertical integration eliminates double marginalization in the full ownership case. However, vertical integration can also have anticompetitive effects including foreclosure and raising rivals' costs. Crawford, Lee, Whinston, and Yurukoglu (2018) provide a comprehensive welfare analysis incorporating both positive and negative effects under partial vertical integration.

Bizzarri (2024) formally considers common ownership in input-output networks. Efficiency would require decreasing coordination among horizontally related firms while maximizing coordination between vertically related firms, but the network structure creates a trade-off between these goals. As a result, common ownership across firms with different network positions can have very heterogeneous effects on consumer welfare.

### 6.3 Labor Market Power and General Equilibrium

Azar, Marinescu, and Steinbaum (2021) develop a general equilibrium framework in which oligopolistic firms have market power over both products and labor. Common ownership enhances market power, leading to wage markdowns and, in a one-sector model, declines in employment, real wages, and the labor share. Azar and Vives (2021) extended this model and find that common ownership leads to lower equilibrium wages, real interest rates, output, labor share, and capital share.

Azar, Marinescu, and Steinbaum (2022) provide empirical evidence: common ownership more than doubled in U.S. labor markets over the last quarter century. Plausibly exogenous increases in common ownership lead to a decrease in average annual earnings per employee at local competitors, with stronger effects in local labor markets with higher S&P 500 employment shares or lower union coverage rates.

Azar and Vives (2021) also reveal a pro-competitive general equilibrium effect of inter-industry common ownership: provided the elasticity of labor supply is high relative to the elasticity of substitution in product markets, an increase in inter-industry common ownership can lead to greater output and lower product market prices. Azar and Vives (2021) use airline data and find that intra-industry common ownership increases prices, but inter-industry common ownership reduces prices.

### 6.4 Endogenous Ownership

Although ownership is generally assumed to be exogenous, Piccolo and others (2020) develop a framework to explore how financial markets shape the ownership structure of industry rivals. When investors influence competition, the return of diversified portfolios and the risk of undiversified portfolios may increase with the industry's degree of common ownership. This crowding out of undiversified investors is shown to exacerbate the anticompetitive effects of common ownership.

## 7. Common Ownership in Antitrust Practice

### 7.1 Different Types of Common Ownership

The degree to which common ownership affects competition depends on the interest and attention of investors to competition between their portfolio companies, as well as the mix of different types of investors in a given company.

**Mutual funds and passive investors** have grown dramatically over recent decades and are responsible for much of the increase in observed common ownership. The fear that these types of investors exercise influence over portfolio firms is what has motivated much of the recent debate.

**Activist investors** may invest in multiple competitors and thereby create or increase common ownership. These cases may be inherently more problematic because activist investors have stated goals of influencing company strategy. Amel-Zadeh and others (2022) show that activists hold surprisingly diversified portfolios, frequently comprising multiple firms in the same industry, and contribute substantially to common ownership.

**Private equity investors** typically take an active role in shaping portfolio firm strategies and take larger ownership stakes, often in privately held firms. Private equity has come under increasing antitrust scrutiny for "roll-up" strategies that may combine rival firms. Depending on the size of ownership interests and control structure, private equity transactions may be analyzed like full mergers.

### 7.2 Common Ownership in Merger Review

The 2010 Horizontal Merger Guidelines outlined the agencies' analysis of partial acquisitions. The 2023 Merger Guidelines expand this to "acquisitions involving partial ownership and minority interests" and explicitly address both cross-ownership (holding a non-controlling interest in a competitor) and common ownership (investors holding non-controlling interests in firms with competitive relationships). Acquisitions of shares are exempt from premerger review if they are solely for investment and the acquiring person will hold 10% or less of outstanding voting securities.

The European Commission has taken common ownership into account in merger review. In its Dow/DuPont decision (2017), it analyzed common ownership links as part of the context of the proposed merger. However, the presence of common ownership in general has ambiguous effects on the competitive impact of mergers, and common ownership concerns are likely of second order compared to analysis of the merger itself.

The most active area where common ownership comes to bear on merger review is private equity deals. Given the complex structure of private equity transactions, economists typically account for ownership in merger analysis using the MHHI, or an ownership-modified version of upward pricing pressure indices.

### 7.3 Legal Challenges to Consummated Common Ownership

Legal scholars weighed in on the implications of the early empirical findings shortly after publication. Elhauge (2016) argues that stock acquisitions creating anticompetitive common ownership ("horizontal shareholdings") are illegal under current antitrust laws. Scott Morton and Hovenkamp (2018) find that both Clayton Act Section 7 and Sherman Act Section 1 could potentially be brought to bear, and note that Section 7 may not require plaintiffs to pinpoint an exact mechanism of harm, as long as there is evidence for likely anticompetitive effects. However, commentators have also pointed to challenges for antitrust lawsuits against common ownership, including the incremental nature of the investments and courts' potential reluctance to hold index funds liable.

**Texas v. BlackRock et al. (2024–present)** is the first case to bring a Section 7 claim against institutional investors holding relatively small ownership stakes. The plaintiff states bring both Section 7 and Section 1 claims against BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street over their ownership of publicly traded U.S. coal producers. The defendants' combined ownership stakes in the nine coal companies range from 8% to 34%.

The Complaint argues that if the three commonly owned SPRB coal producers were to outright merge, the increase in HHI would be above 2,000 and the combined entity would account for over 60% of the market — presumptively unlawful under the 2023 Merger Guidelines. The Complaint supports its allegation with: (i) statements made by commonly owned coal companies in investor calls discussing output restriction linked to ESG priorities; (ii) production data showing commonly owned coal companies reduced production while privately held companies did not, while prices increased; (iii) profit data showing profits increased even as production decreased.

The Court denied the defendants' motion to dismiss on August 1, 2025. The DOJ and FTC filed a statement of interest affirming that the "solely for investment" exception does not provide bright-line protection to passive minority investors without further analysis. The agencies confirmed that antitrust safe harbors protect typical index fund investing and beneficial governance advocacy, but do not protect the use of commonly managed stock in competitors to encourage market-wide output reductions. Evidence of anticompetitive effects appears to be the dividing line between unproblematic and potentially unlawful asset manager behavior.

### 7.4 Enforcement Against Director Interlocks

Director interlocks are the common ownership mechanism most clearly addressed in U.S. antitrust law. Section 8 of the Clayton Act generally prohibits any person from serving as a director or officer in competing firms on a per se basis (without requiring proof of actual anticompetitive effects). Removing interlocking directors is relatively straightforward, though may destroy efficiencies.

In 2022, then-AAG Jonathan Kanter called Section 8 "an important, but underenforced, part of our antitrust laws" and announced an extensive review of interlocking directorates. The DOJ has since announced several director resignations in response to its concerns. Interlocking directors also featured in the DOJ's case against Silicon Valley companies for alleged non-poach agreements, where academic research found that connections between Apple, Google, and other high-tech companies via shared directors may have facilitated communications to reduce competition for certain workers.

## 8. Conclusion

The common ownership hypothesis has policy implications that go above and beyond whether specific cases or industries warrant antitrust enforcement. Given the challenges associated with bringing antitrust cases, should lawmakers pass laws to limit common ownership? Should they prohibit communication between investors and portfolio companies? Should they regulate management compensation to prohibit structures that stifle competition?

These questions point to the broader "trilemma" raised by the common ownership hypothesis: portfolio diversification, shareholder representation, and product market competition are fundamentally in tension with each other. It is impossible to prioritize all three goals simultaneously; to perfectly achieve two inevitably compromises the third. The rise of common ownership and increased concentration in the asset manager industry suggest that the balance has shifted toward financial diversification and shareholder control, potentially at the cost of competition.

Distributional considerations also play a role: consumers at the lower end of the income distribution arguably benefit more from product market competition than from diversifying or protecting their (non-existent) investment portfolios. Thus, the common ownership hypothesis connects antitrust policy with larger macroeconomic and societal questions that antitrust enforcement alone is not fully equipped to address.

## Technical Appendix

Denoting the control weights of shareholder $o$ in firm $i$ as $\gamma_{io}$ and the ownership share of firm $i$ accruing to shareholder $o$ as $\beta_{io}$, firm $i$'s objective under the common ownership hypothesis is to maximize:

$$\sum_{o} \gamma_{io} \sum_{j} \beta_{jo} \pi_{j}$$

where the first sum is over all shareholders of the firm, and the second sum is over all other firms in which its shareholders have financial interests. This objective function is equivalent to maximizing:

$$\pi_{i} + \sum_{j \neq i} \kappa_{ij} \pi_{j}$$

where $\pi_i$ is the profit of firm $i$ and $\kappa_{ij}$ is the **profit weight** that firm $i$ places on (its competitor) $j$'s profits:

$$\kappa_{ij} = \frac{\sum_{o} \gamma_{io} \beta_{jo}}{\sum_{o} \gamma_{io} \beta_{io}}$$

The ownership shares $\beta_{io}$ are generally determined by the fraction of outstanding shares held by a given shareholder. Most empirical studies assume proportional control ($\gamma_{io} = \beta_{io}$), motivated by the "one share, one vote" rule.

The **Modified HHI (MHHI)**, developed by O'Brien and Salop (2000), expands the standard HHI to account for this objective function:

$$\textit{MHHI} = \textit{HHI} + \sum_{i} \sum_{j \neq i} \kappa_{ij} s_j s_i$$

where $s_i$ and $s_j$ are market shares. The second term — the "MHHI delta" — is the sum of the product of competitor market shares, weighted by the profit weights. This term captures the additional "virtual" concentration arising from common ownership incentives. Backus, Conlon, and Sinkinson (2021) and Ederer and Pellegrino (2026) show that different control weights corresponding to different models of corporate governance do not substantially change the conclusions about the rise of common ownership among U.S. public companies.
