# Trust and Promises over Time

**Florian Ederer** (Boston University; CEPR, ECGI, and NBER)
**Frédéric Schneider** (ETH Zürich)

*American Economic Journal: Microeconomics*, Vol. 14, No. 4, pp. 304–336, November 2022

---

## Abstract

We investigate whether promises to cooperate are more credible when there is a delay between making the promise and keeping it. In a laboratory trust game, we find that communication increases cooperation rates substantially, while the length of the delay between promise and action has no significant effect. Promises are kept at rates well above what would be predicted by standard game-theoretic reasoning. Our results suggest that promises function as credible commitment devices that are remarkably robust to the passage of time over periods of up to three weeks.

---

## Experimental Design

### The Trust Game

The experiment employs a standard two-player trust game:

- **Trustor** decides between **OUT** (secure outside option) and **IN** (entrusting money to the trustee).
- **Trustee** observes whether IN was chosen, then decides between **ROLL** (cooperation, expanding the pie) and **DON'T ROLL** (defection, keeping a larger personal share).

Payoffs are designed so that ROLL is the socially efficient but individually costly action for the trustee. A trustee acting in narrow self-interest would choose DON'T ROLL, anticipating which the trustor should choose OUT.

### 2×3 Design

The experiment crosses two communication conditions with three timing conditions:

**Communication conditions:**
- **COMM:** Before the trustee learns whether IN or OUT was chosen, the trustee sends a free-form message to the trustor. The trustor then decides IN/OUT.
- **NOCOMM:** No message is sent; the trustor decides IN/OUT without any communication from the trustee.

**Timing conditions:**
- **IMM (Immediate):** Trustee chooses ROLL/DON'T ROLL immediately after the trustor's decision.
- **EARLY:** There is a delay of approximately one week between the trustor's decision and the trustee's action.
- **LATE:** There is a delay of approximately three weeks between the trustor's decision and the trustee's action.

This yields six treatment cells: COMM×IMM, COMM×EARLY, COMM×LATE, NOCOMM×IMM, NOCOMM×EARLY, NOCOMM×LATE.

### Sample

707 subjects participated in the experiment at the University of Zurich and Yale University. Subjects were recruited for a multi-session study, with sessions separated by the designated delay intervals.

---

## Results

### Effect of Communication on Cooperation

The main finding is a large and significant effect of communication on trustee cooperation:

**COMM raises the ROLL rate by approximately 1.80 (on a 0–10 scale), a statistically significant increase.** This represents roughly a 50% increase in cooperation relative to the NOCOMM baseline.

The effect of communication is robust across all three timing conditions and is not significantly moderated by the length of the delay.

### Effect of Delay on Cooperation

**There is no significant difference in ROLL rates across the IMM, EARLY, and LATE conditions.** Promises made three weeks before the action are kept at rates statistically indistinguishable from promises made immediately before the action.

This finding contradicts a naive intuition that promises decay over time. Instead, the commitment value of a promise appears remarkably durable.

### Promises vs. Non-Promises in the COMM Condition

Within the COMM treatment, the trustee's message can be coded as containing an explicit promise (approximately 66–75% of COMM trustees made such a promise) or not.

- Trustees who made explicit promises had a ROLL rate of approximately **6.2** (out of 10).
- Trustees who communicated but did not make an explicit promise had a ROLL rate of approximately **4.2**.

This difference is substantial and confirms that it is the promise itself — not mere communication — that drives the elevated cooperation rates.

### Beliefs and Communication

Communication also affects trustors' beliefs about trustee behavior:

- Communication raises all belief measures (probability that the trustee will ROLL) by approximately **20 percentage points**.
- This belief update is **persistent across delays**: trustors in COMM conditions at EARLY and LATE timing hold beliefs similar to those in COMM×IMM.

The belief effects track the actual ROLL rates, suggesting that trustors correctly anticipate the cooperation-enhancing effect of promises and its robustness to delay.

### IN Rates

Trustors' willingness to choose IN is significantly higher in COMM conditions than in NOCOMM conditions, consistent with trustors correctly internalizing that communication raises the probability of ROLL.

---

## Conclusion

Communication raises cooperation in the trust game by approximately 50%, and this effect persists over delays of up to three weeks. Promises function as credible commitment devices that are remarkably stable across time horizons. The results suggest that informal enforcement mechanisms — moral costs of breaking explicit promises — operate over extended time horizons and can sustain cooperation in sequential interactions where there is a gap between commitment and action.
