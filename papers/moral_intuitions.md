# Moral Intuitions of Promise Keeping

**Florian Ederer** (Boston University; CEPR, ECGI, and NBER)
**Alexander Stremitzer** (ETH Zürich)

*Principia*, Special Issue on Heuristics, Ethics and Epistemology of Law, Vol. LXV, pp. 5–33, December 2018

---

## Abstract

We investigate how ordinary people understand and follow through on promises by connecting experimental evidence on promise-keeping behavior to the doctrines of contract law. We examine how promise-keeping varies with the mutuality of promises, the dynamics of promising and performance over time, income levels, and whether obligations arise from continuous or discrete performance decisions. We find that lay moral intuitions about promise-keeping align closely with established contract law principles, suggesting that these legal doctrines reflect underlying moral norms that can be identified through experimental methods.

**JEL Codes:** C91, D03, K12.

---

## Motivation

Contract law is built on a set of doctrines governing when promises are enforceable, what obligations they create, and how they interact over time. These doctrines — covering mutuality of obligation, substantial performance, divisibility, and contract formation — are typically justified by appeals to moral intuitions about the nature and force of promises.

This paper investigates whether these intuitions are empirically grounded: do ordinary people's moral judgments about promise-keeping track the distinctions that contract law draws? If so, legal doctrines about promises may be understood as codifications of widely shared moral norms, with implications for both the justification of these doctrines and the design of default contract rules.

---

## Research Questions

The paper examines several dimensions of promise-keeping behavior that correspond to specific contract law doctrines:

### 1. Mutual Promises (Mutuality of Obligation)

Contract law generally requires mutuality of obligation: for a contract to be enforceable, both parties must be bound. The paper studies whether laypeople similarly regard mutual promises as more binding than unilateral ones.

**Finding:** Subjects treat mutual promises as more morally binding than unilateral promises. When both parties have made commitments, subjects are more likely to follow through on their own promise even when it is costly to do so.

### 2. Dynamics of Promising and Performance Over Time

Contracts often extend over time, and the timing of performance relative to the original promise matters for its moral force. The paper studies whether the perceived obligation to keep a promise weakens as time passes between the making of the promise and the required performance.

**Finding:** Consistent with the "Trust and Promises over Time" paper (Ederer and Schneider, 2022), moral intuitions about promise-keeping are remarkably robust to the passage of time. Subjects do not substantially discount the obligation to fulfill a promise made earlier, even over extended time horizons.

### 3. Income and Wealth Effects

The paper explores whether the willingness to keep costly promises varies with subjects' income or wealth levels, examining whether promise-keeping is a normal good.

**Finding:** Promise-keeping behavior does not vary strongly with income levels in the experimental setting, suggesting that the moral obligation of promise-keeping operates broadly across economic circumstances rather than being concentrated among wealthier or more economically secure subjects.

### 4. Continuous vs. Discrete Performance

Contract law distinguishes between obligations that can be performed incrementally (continuous performance, such as ongoing services) and those requiring a single act (discrete performance, such as delivery of goods). The paper studies whether subjects' moral intuitions track this distinction.

**Finding:** Subjects show different patterns of promise fulfillment for continuous vs. discrete obligations. Continuous performance creates a more gradual sense of obligation that may be easier to partially satisfy (substantial performance doctrine), while discrete performance is treated as an all-or-nothing obligation.

---

## Connection to Contract Law Doctrines

The paper draws explicit connections between the experimental findings and four contract law doctrines:

**Mutuality doctrine:** The requirement that both parties be bound by a contract reflects the moral intuition that mutual promises are more binding. Experiments confirm that people feel more obligated when their counterpart is also bound.

**Substantial performance:** Contract law allows recovery even for imperfect performance if substantial compliance with the contract has been achieved. Subjects' willingness to accept partial performance for continuous obligations is consistent with this doctrine.

**Divisible obligations:** A contract may be divisible into independent units, each of which can be performed and compensated separately. Subjects treat divisible obligations as creating distinct, separable moral duties rather than a single all-or-nothing commitment.

**Contract formation:** The requirement that offers and acceptances create mutual expectations before a binding contract arises is consistent with subjects' stronger obligations when expectations are clearly established through bilateral communication.

---

## Implications for Law and Economics

The paper argues that experimental evidence on moral intuitions can inform the design of default contract rules. If legal doctrines closely track laypeople's moral intuitions:

1. **Default rules should be intuition-conforming:** Parties who do not specify contractual terms can be governed by defaults that match their likely moral expectations, reducing the need for costly specification of all contingencies.

2. **Deviations from intuition-conforming rules impose costs:** When legal doctrines deviate from moral intuitions (e.g., requiring a formal promise in writing to create legal obligation), they create a gap between moral and legal obligation that may be costly to navigate.

3. **Heterogeneity in intuitions requires caution:** While average intuitions align with existing doctrines, there is variation across subjects that may warrant tailoring default rules to particular contexts or populations.

---

## Conclusion

Ordinary people's moral intuitions about promise-keeping are consistent with the major doctrines of contract law. Mutual promises are more binding than unilateral ones; the obligation to keep promises is robust over time; and continuous obligations are treated differently from discrete ones. These findings support the view that contract law codifies genuine moral norms about promising, providing both a positive account of the origins of legal doctrines and normative guidance for the design of default rules in contract law.
