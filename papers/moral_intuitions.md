# Moral Intuitions of Promise Keeping

**Florian Ederer** (Boston University; CEPR, ECGI, and NBER)
**Alexander Stremitzer** (ETH Zurich)

*Principia*, Special Issue on Heuristics, Ethics and Epistemology of Law, Vol. LXV, pp. 5–33, December 2018

---

## Abstract

We present experimental evidence on lay people's moral intuitions about promise keeping and relate them to the doctrines of contract law. Our results illuminate the conditions under which people feel morally bound to keep promises — in particular, how promise-keeping varies with the mutuality of promises, the dynamics of promising over time, the continuous vs. binary nature of the required performance, the promisor's income, and whether the promisor received the promisee's counter-promise. We find that lay moral intuitions track established contract law doctrines: the mutuality requirement, the substantial performance doctrine, the divisible obligations doctrine, and the contract formation doctrine. The common law mailbox rule, however, departs from moral intuition in a direction that civil law systems' receipt requirement corrects.

**Keywords:** Contracts, Promises.

## 1. Experimental Design

We conduct two experiments with 107 subjects (UCLA undergraduates) at CASSEL, in February and March 2012.

### Experiment I (Binary Performance, $n = 74$, 42 women)

Subject A (the dictator/promisor) sends a pre-coded message to subject B (the promisee): either "I promise to choose Roll" or "I do NOT promise to choose Roll." Messages are delivered with 50% probability, independently of A's choice. Roles are assigned randomly. Subjects play 2 practice rounds and 8 paying rounds in matching groups of at least 10. Show-up fees were \$5 (Session 1) and \$10 (Session 2). The experiment was run with z-Tree.

Payoffs: If A chooses Roll, A receives \$10 and B receives \$12 with probability 5/6 (\$0 with probability 1/6). If A chooses Don't Roll, A receives \$14 and B receives \$0.

### Experiment II (Continuous Performance, $n = 33$, 14 women)

A chooses from a finer action set: Don't Roll, $1/4$ Roll, $1/2$ Roll, $3/4$ Roll, Roll. A never receives B's message. Show-up fee: \$5. First-order beliefs are elicited via a 5-point scale.

## 2. Results

### 2.1 Mutuality of Promises

Contract law's mutuality requirement holds that "unless both are bound, neither is bound." We test this by varying whether A received a counter-promise from B.

When A had promised, receiving B's counter-promise raised A's Roll rate from 21% (no counter-promise) to 49% ($p = 0.01$). However, receiving B's counter-promise (49%) did not significantly differ from B sending no message (45%, $p = 0.44$). A large drop occurs from no message (45%) to B's explicit non-promise (21%, $p = 0.01$). Dictators **punish explicit refusal** by B but do not substantially reward a counter-promise per se. This partially supports the mutuality doctrine — promisors feel less bound when the other party explicitly refuses — but gratuitous promises are not fully rejected.

### 2.2 Dynamics of Promising

We test whether the inclination to promise and the inclination to keep promises evolve over time (H2a: promising increases over time in Exp I; H2b: promising decreases over time in Exp II).

Average promising rates were 76% (Exp I) and 51% (Exp II). In Experiment I (binary), promising increased significantly from 66% in period 1 to 82% in period 2 ($p < 0.01$), supporting H2a. In Experiment II (continuous), promising decreased from 61% to 36% ($p = 0.03$), supporting H2b. Roll/performance rates did not increase significantly over time in either experiment ($p = 0.48$, Exp I; $p = 0.26$, Exp II).

### 2.3 Continuous vs. Binary Performance

The substantial performance doctrine in contract law allows recovery even for imperfect performance; the divisible obligations doctrine treats a divisible contract as a series of independent obligations.

In Experiment I (binary), 44% of promisors chose Roll. In Experiment II (continuous), 62% of promisors contributed at least something, significantly above the 10% threshold ($p = 0.09$), supporting H3a that promisors are more likely to provide at least partial performance when the action set is continuous. The weighted contribution rate was 46% (Exp II) versus 44% (Exp I, $p = 0.85$), so H3c (equal total contribution) is not rejected. These results support H3b: promisors are more likely to give something under continuous performance but give less on average, consistent with the substantial performance doctrine.

### 2.4 Income Effects

We test whether promise-keeping varies with the promisor's income (H4), using subjects' self-reported income categories.

Promise-keeping was strongly increasing in income. Among "Affluent" subjects, 77% kept their promise. Among "Very Poor" subjects, only 6% kept their promise. Most pairwise differences across income groups are statistically significant. The income effect is not driven by generally higher pro-social attitudes among wealthier subjects (social desirability scores do not differ). This finding supports the statute of frauds, which imposes formal requirements for high-value contracts: the lower promise-keeping rates among poorer subjects suggests formal enforcement is more important when informal moral sanctions are weaker. 

### 2.5 Receipt of Promise

The common law mailbox rule holds that a promise is binding upon dispatch (before receipt), whereas civil law systems typically require receipt. We test whether moral intuition aligns with dispatch or receipt (H5).

In Experiment I (binary), Roll rates were 46% (received B's message) versus 41% (did not receive), a non-significant difference ($p = 0.57$). In Experiment II (continuous), contribution rates were 73% (received) versus 53% (not received), a significant difference ($p = 0.04$). H5 is thus supported for continuous but not binary performance. Moral intuitions favor a receipt requirement — tracking civil law rather than common law — particularly when partial performance is possible.

### 2.6 Mechanism: Conditional Guilt Aversion

To assess whether expectations drive promise-keeping, we test whether B's expectations affect A's behavior when A has not made a promise. The effect of B's expectations without a promise is small and non-significant (Roll rates 6% vs. 11%, $p = 0.23$). This is consistent with conditional guilt aversion: expectations affect behavior only when activated by a promise, not unconditionally. The guilt aversion mechanism thus requires a promissory link to operate.

## 3. Implications for Contract Law

- **Mutuality requirement**: Partially supported — explicit refusal by the promisee significantly reduces promise-keeping, but a counter-promise does not significantly raise it above the no-message baseline.
- **Substantial performance doctrine**: Supported — promisors are more likely to provide partial performance when the action space is continuous, consistent with partial satisfaction of obligation being morally meaningful.
- **Divisible obligations**: Supported by the continuous performance results — fractional contributions track a divisible moral obligation framework.
- **Statute of frauds**: Supported — lower promise-keeping rates among low-income subjects justify formal requirements for high-stakes contracts where informal moral enforcement is weaker.
- **Mailbox rule vs. receipt requirement**: Moral intuitions favor receipt (civil law) over dispatch (common law) for continuous obligations.
