# Feedback and Motivation in Dynamic Tournaments

**Florian Ederer** (Boston University; CEPR, ECGI, and NBER)

*Journal of Economics and Management Strategy*, Vol. 19, No. 3, pp. 733–769, Fall 2010

---

## Abstract

This paper studies the effects of interim performance feedback on effort in dynamic tournaments. When an organization reveals agents' relative performance between stages of a multi-stage competition, it triggers competing incentive effects: workers learn where they stand (their relative position) and update their beliefs about their relative productivity. The evaluation effect and motivation effect of feedback work in opposite directions for agents in different positions, and the net impact on total effort and selection efficiency depends on the distribution of performance gaps and whether agent ability affects the marginal return to effort.

**JEL Codes:** D82, J31, J41, M12, M52.

---

## Model

### The Dynamic Tournament

Two risk-neutral agents compete in a two-stage tournament for a fixed prize. In each stage $t = 1, 2$, agent $i$ chooses effort $e_t^i$. Output in stage $t$ is:

$$x_t^i = e_t^i + \varepsilon_t^i$$

where $\varepsilon_t^i$ is a noise term. The agent with the highest total output $x_1^i + x_2^i$ wins the prize.

### Feedback Decision

After stage 1, the principal observes the performance gap $\Delta \equiv x_1^A - x_1^B$ and decides whether to reveal it to the agents before they choose stage-2 effort. The principal's feedback policy is a mapping from the observed gap to a signal sent to the agents.

### Effort Responses to Feedback

When feedback reveals the gap $\Delta$, the stage-2 effort of each agent depends on the posterior probability that each agent will win. A larger gap implies one agent has a higher probability of winning the tournament, which affects both agents' incentives:

- **The leader** has a comfortable advantage and may reduce effort (complacency effect): the marginal return to effort is lower when the probability of winning is already high.
- **The laggard** faces a large deficit and may reduce effort (discouragement effect): the marginal return to effort is lower when the probability of winning is very low.
- **Neck-and-neck agents** (small $|\Delta|$) face the most competitive situation: both agents have a meaningful probability of winning, generating the highest second-stage effort.

## Main Results

### Evaluation Effect and Motivation Effect

Feedback generates two competing effects:

**Evaluation effect:** Feedback reveals agents' relative standing in the tournament, allowing each agent to condition stage-2 effort on an informative signal about where the competition stands. This improves effort allocation across stages.

**Motivation effect:** If agent ability affects the marginal return to effort — so that agents also learn something about relative productivity from interim feedback — the signal has additional effects on effort. A leader may also learn that she is more productive, potentially increasing effort; a laggard may learn the opposite.

### Effect of Feedback on Total Effort

The net effect of feedback on total expected effort depends on the interplay of these effects and the distribution of performance gaps after stage 1:

- **Symmetric agents (no ability differences):** Feedback unambiguously reduces effort for agents with large gaps. Whether it increases average effort depends on the fraction of pairings that are neck-and-neck. For many noise distributions, feedback reduces expected total effort compared to no feedback.

- **Asymmetric agents (ability differences):** Feedback can increase total effort if the ability gap is informative and agents' marginal returns to effort rise with ability.

### Selection Efficiency

Even when feedback reduces total effort, it may improve **selection efficiency** — the probability that the more productive agent wins the tournament. By allowing agents to observe the gap, feedback enables the tournament to more accurately identify the better worker.

The principal's optimal feedback policy balances the total-effort cost of feedback against the selection-efficiency benefit:

- **When selection matters more than total effort:** Provide full feedback. Revealing the gap helps the better agent establish a commanding lead and win, reducing the probability of a selection error.
- **When total effort matters more than selection:** Suppress feedback or provide coarser information. Keeping agents uncertain about their relative standing maintains competitive pressure and prevents discouragement.

### Optimal Feedback Policy

The principal designs a feedback policy — which aspects of interim performance to reveal — to maximize the expected value of the tournament. The optimal policy is generally not binary (full disclosure vs. no disclosure) but may involve coarse signals that preserve competitive pressure while providing some information.

Key comparative statics:

- **More complementarity between effort and ability:** The motivation effect strengthens, making feedback more likely to increase total effort.
- **Higher noise in performance:** More noise reduces the informativeness of feedback and weakens both the evaluation and motivation effects.
- **Stronger discouragement aversion:** If agents are more sensitive to falling far behind, the cost of revealing large gaps is higher, shifting the optimal policy toward less disclosure.

---

## Conclusion

Interim performance feedback in dynamic tournaments has competing effects on incentives. While feedback improves effort allocation for neck-and-neck agents, it discourages lagging agents and may reduce total effort for agents who are far apart. Organizations face a fundamental trade-off between using feedback to sustain competitive pressure versus concealing it to prevent discouragement. The optimal feedback policy depends on whether the organization's primary objective is to maximize total effort or to ensure that the more productive agent wins — goals that often pull in opposite directions.
