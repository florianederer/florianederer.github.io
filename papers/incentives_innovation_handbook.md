# Incentives for Innovation: Bankruptcy, Corporate Governance, and Compensation Systems

**Florian Ederer** (University of California at Los Angeles)
**Gustavo Manso** (Massachusetts Institute of Technology)

In: Robert E. Litan (ed.), *Handbook of Law, Innovation, and Growth*, Chapter 4, Edward Elgar Publishing, 2011

---

## Overview

This chapter surveys the economics literature on incentives and innovation, with a focus on optimal contracting theory and its applications to bankruptcy law, corporate governance, and executive and employee compensation systems. The central theme is that standard pay-for-performance contracts, while effective at eliciting effort on routine tasks, may be poorly suited to innovation — which requires experimentation, tolerance of early failure, and long-run commitment.

---

## 1. Foundations

### 1.1 Economics of Incentives

The canonical principal-agent model (Ross 1973, Holmstrom 1979) shows that pay-for-performance contracts increase effort at the cost of imposing risk on risk-averse agents. Dynamic extensions (Rogerson 1985, Holmstrom and Milgrom 1987, Sannikov 2008) show that optimal dynamic contracts integrate information efficiently over time. Multi-task models (Holmstrom and Milgrom 1991) demonstrate that when agents perform both routine and innovative tasks, high-powered incentives on measurable outputs distort effort allocation away from harder-to-measure activities, including innovation.

### 1.2 Incentives for Innovation

Innovation presents unique challenges for incentive design. Holmstrom (1989) argues that the noisiness of innovative activities makes standard performance measurement costly, so optimal contracts for research workers provide weaker pay-for-performance incentives than contracts for routine workers. Aghion and Tirole (1994) analyze how the allocation of property rights and control over research projects determines innovation incentives in incomplete contract settings.

Manso (2009) develops the first principal-agent model that explicitly incorporates the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. In Manso's model, the optimal contract for exploration **tolerates early failure and rewards long-run success**: because innovative projects require agents to experiment and learn, early failures are informative rather than indicative of low effort, and punishing them discourages the very behavior the principal seeks to induce. The optimal exploration contract can be implemented through stock options (which reward only long-run success), golden parachutes (which reduce the punishment for early failure), and entrenchment provisions (which protect the agent from early termination). Ederer (2009) extends this framework to settings with parallel innovation by multiple agents and shows that optimal contracts for parallel innovation combine individual and group incentives, rewarding long-term joint success.

### 1.3 Experimental Evidence

The conflict between pay-for-performance and creative problem-solving has been documented in psychology. Glucksberg (1962) showed that subjects offered financial rewards for solving the candle problem — which requires functional insight — performed worse than those offered no rewards. Subsequent work by McCullers, McGraw, Kohn, and Amabile confirmed that pay-for-performance undermines performance on creative tasks while improving performance on routine ones.

Ederer and Manso (2009) bring this question to the laboratory with a controlled economic experiment. Using both a gold prospecting task (which requires exploration of uncertain technologies) and a lemonade stand task (which permits both exploitation and exploration strategies), they show that **exploration contracts — which tolerate early failure and reward long-run success — substantially outperform standard pay-for-performance and fixed-wage contracts in eliciting innovative behavior**. This confirms the theoretical prediction that the structure of incentives, not merely their level, is the key driver of innovative output.

## 2. Applications

### 2.1 Bankruptcy Law

Bankruptcy law determines the consequences of entrepreneurial failure and thereby shapes innovation incentives. Historical legal systems were highly punitive: ancient Roman law imposed debt slavery, and British statutes of 1542 and 1570 provided for imprisonment of debtors. Modern bankruptcy systems vary considerably in how they treat failed entrepreneurs. The US Chapter 11 procedure allows for reorganization and fresh starts, while European bankruptcy codes have historically been more creditor-friendly, imposing larger personal costs on failed debtors.

Applying Manso's (2009) framework, the optimal bankruptcy regime for promoting exploration and entrepreneurship is a **fresh-start law** that limits the personal consequences of failure, effectively implementing the tolerance for failure that the optimal exploration contract requires. In contrast, creditor-friendly bankruptcy codes that impose severe penalties on debtors implement something closer to the optimal exploitation contract.

Acharya and Subramanian (2009) provide empirical evidence consistent with this prediction: industries in countries with more creditor-friendly bankruptcy codes file 9.7% fewer patents and receive 13.3% fewer citations, with the effect concentrated in innovative industries. The European Charter for Small Enterprises explicitly recognizes that "failure is concomitant with responsible initiative" and advocates for second-chance opportunities for failed entrepreneurs.

### 2.2 CEO Compensation

Executive compensation is a central mechanism through which corporate governance shapes innovation incentives. Stock options are particularly prevalent in innovative industries: media sector CEOs receive approximately 86.9% of their compensation from options, compared with 19.3% in old-economy firms (Lambert and Larcker 2004).

The standard critique of executive pay focuses on option repricing — the practice of lowering exercise prices when stock falls — as a "reward for failure" (Carter and Lynch 2001). From the perspective of innovation incentives, however, repricing can be optimal: it prevents early failure from permanently destroying the manager's incentive to continue experimenting. More broadly, Manso's (2009) framework implies that the optimal CEO compensation contract for innovative firms should combine **stock options** (concentrated rewards for long-run success), **golden parachutes** (limited punishment for failure), and **entrenchment provisions** (protection from early termination).

### 2.3 Management of R&D Workers

The appropriate management of research and development workers has evolved substantially. Early 20th century Taylorist management emphasized close supervision and performance monitoring. The experience of innovative firms suggests that tolerance for failure and long-term commitment are essential for creative work.

The 3M Corporation provides a celebrated example. CEO William McKnight's management philosophy explicitly embraced experimentation and accepted mistakes: "Mistakes will be made. But if a person is essentially right, the mistakes he or she makes are not as serious in the long run as the mistakes management will make if it undertakes to tell those in authority exactly how they must do their jobs." This culture produced transformative innovations including Post-it Notes, which emerged from a failed attempt to develop a strong adhesive. Similarly, Thomas Watson of IBM, when an executive feared dismissal after a ten-million-dollar mistake, reportedly responded: "You can't be serious. We have just spent ten million dollars educating you."

Academic tenure serves as an institutional commitment device that implements the tolerance for failure required by optimal exploration contracts: by guaranteeing long-term employment, tenure allows researchers to take risks without fear of early termination. Azoulay, Graff-Zivin, and Manso (2009) compare Howard Hughes Medical Institute (HHMI) investigators — who receive five-year appointments and face limited consequences for early failure — with scientists funded by the National Institutes of Health under standard grant mechanisms. HHMI-funded scientists produce significantly more innovative (highly cited and unconventional) research, consistent with the prediction that failure-tolerant funding mechanisms promote exploration.

Acharya, Baghai, Wadji, and Subramanian (2009) document that firms in countries with more stringent dismissal laws file 14.9% more patents annually, suggesting that employment protection — by reducing the threat of termination — functions similarly to golden parachutes in enabling innovative risk-taking. The debate over non-compete clauses in employment contracts relates directly to the same mechanism: non-competes may discourage employees from developing human capital specific to innovative projects if they cannot capture returns by moving to competitors.

## 3. Conclusion

The economic analysis of innovation incentives reveals a fundamental tension between standard optimal contracting insights and the demands of creative work. Pay-for-performance contracts that are optimal for effort elicitation on routine tasks may actively discourage the exploration, experimentation, and tolerance of early failure that innovation requires. Optimal incentive design for innovative activities — whether implemented through bankruptcy law, corporate governance, or compensation systems — should reward long-run success, limit the consequences of early failure, and provide long-term commitment. Substantial additional research is needed to quantify the impact of these different policy levers on innovative output and welfare.
