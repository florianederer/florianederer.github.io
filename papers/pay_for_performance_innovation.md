# Is Pay-for-Performance Detrimental to Innovation?

**Florian Ederer** (Boston University; CEPR, ECGI, and NBER)
**Gustavo Manso** (University of California, Berkeley; NBER)

*Management Science*, Vol. 59, No. 7, pp. 1496–1513, July 2013

---

## Abstract

We study the effects of pay-for-performance on exploration and innovation. We show theoretically that innovation requires tolerating early failure and rewarding long-term success. Unlike standard pay-for-performance schemes, which punish early failure, optimal contracts for innovation combine termination threats with golden parachutes or provide compensation through stock options that reward long-term success. We test these predictions using two laboratory experiments in which subjects perform a real-effort innovation task. We find that pay-for-performance is indeed detrimental to innovation compared with contracts that reward long-term success and tolerate early failure.

**JEL Codes:** D86, M52, O31.

## Theoretical Framework

### The Innovation Problem

The key insight is that innovation — unlike routine production — requires **exploration**: trying unfamiliar approaches that may fail in the short run but reveal superior methods in the long run. An optimal exploration strategy tolerates early failure (to incentivize risk-taking) and rewards long-term success (to maintain effort throughout).

Standard pay-for-performance (PFP) schemes link compensation directly to each period's output. This creates a bias toward **exploitation** of known approaches (which succeed reliably in the short run) and against **exploration** of novel approaches (which may fail early even if they are superior in expectation).

### Optimal Contracts for Innovation

The model characterizes optimal contracts under two paradigms:

**Pay-for-performance (exploitation contract):** Compensates primarily based on current output. Under PFP, agents who explore and fail early are penalized, discouraging exploration.

**Exploration contract:** Tolerates early failure by deferring much of the compensation to the long run. Key features:
1. **Tolerance for early failure:** Low performance in early periods does not terminate employment or severely reduce pay.
2. **Long-term rewards:** High compensation for sustained long-run success, implemented through stock options, deferred compensation, or profit-sharing over later periods.

The exploration contract directly targets the incentive to explore: agents are not punished for failing early while searching for better methods, but are rewarded once they find and exploit a superior approach.

**Termination with golden parachute:** A variant of the exploration contract that fires agents who fail early but compensates them generously upon termination. This retains high-powered incentives for those who stay while not penalizing the type of early failure that is symptomatic of exploration.

## Laboratory Experiments

### Experiment 1: Gold Prospecting Task (CASSEL, UCLA)

#### Design

30 subjects participated in a within-subjects experiment at the CASSEL laboratory at UCLA. The task is a stylized **gold prospecting** problem in which subjects choose between:

- A **known location** (Mountain 1) with a fixed, reliable gold yield — analogous to exploiting a known work method.
- An **unknown location** (Mountain 2 or 3) with uncertain yield — analogous to exploring a new approach.

Subjects were paid under two contracts in counterbalanced order:
- **Pay-for-performance:** Rewarded proportionally to output in each period.
- **Exploration contract:** Rewarded only for output in the final periods (tolerating early failure).

#### Results

At a parameterization of $p = 0.35$ (probability that the unknown location is superior):

- Under the **exploration contract:** 24 out of 30 subjects (80%) chose the risky Mountain 2 in the exploration phase.
- Under **pay-for-performance:** Only 9 out of 30 subjects (30%) chose the risky Mountain 2.

The McNemar test for the within-subjects comparison yields $p = 0.0007$, confirming a highly significant treatment effect.

---

### Experiment 2: Lemonade Stand Task (HBS CLER)

#### Design

379 subjects participated in a between-subjects experiment at the HBS Computer Lab for Experimental Research. The task is a **lemonade stand location** problem in which subjects must discover the profit-maximizing location for a lemonade stand among multiple possible locations. 

The optimal location yields significantly higher profits than all other locations, but subjects do not know which location is optimal. They must explore locations over multiple periods to discover the optimum. This is a genuine exploration task: staying in a suboptimal location (exploitation) is reliable but forfeits the gains from finding the true optimum.

#### Contracts

Subjects were randomly assigned to one of five contracts:

| Contract | Description |
|---|---|
| **Fixed Wage** | Flat payment regardless of output |
| **Pay-for-Performance (PFP)** | 50% of all profits across all periods |
| **Exploration** | 50% of profits in last 10 periods only (early failure tolerated) |
| **Termination** | PFP with employment terminated if early performance falls below threshold |
| **Termination + Golden Parachute** | Termination threat but generous severance upon early termination |

#### Results

Percentage of subjects who find the optimal location:

| Contract | % Finding Optimal Location |
|---|---|
| Exploration | **80%** |
| Fixed Wage | 60% |
| Termination + Golden Parachute | 65% |
| Termination | 45% |
| Pay-for-Performance | **40%** |

The exploration contract achieves the highest discovery rate (80%), while PFP achieves the lowest (40%). The difference between exploration and PFP is large and statistically significant.

The termination contract without a golden parachute (45%) performs worse than even fixed wage, confirming the prediction that termination threats alone discourage exploration unless accompanied by sufficient compensation upon early failure.

---

## Conclusion

Pay-for-performance is detrimental to innovation because it penalizes the early failures that are a natural byproduct of exploration. Contracts that tolerate early failure and reward long-term success — exploration contracts — significantly outperform standard PFP in eliciting innovative behavior. These findings provide a theoretical and experimental foundation for understanding why innovative firms use deferred compensation, stock options, profit-sharing, and other long-horizon incentive schemes: such contracts are not merely rent extraction by managers but are optimal responses to the exploration requirements of innovative activity.
