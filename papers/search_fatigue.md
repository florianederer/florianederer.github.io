*Review of Industrial Organization*, Vol. 54, No. 3, pp. 485–508, May 2019

## Abstract

We study consumer search in markets where searching is not only costly but also tiring. Consumer search fatigue — the reduction in search effectiveness or willingness to continue searching after evaluating multiple products — generates market dynamics that deviate substantially from standard search theory. The model predicts time-varying and cyclical prices, product proliferation as a firm strategy to reduce per-product search intensity, and the voluntary provision of consumer assistance services by firms that benefit from attracting tired searchers. We analyze how search fatigue affects welfare and characterize conditions under which product proliferation and price cycles are most pronounced.

**JEL Codes:** D11, D83, L11, L13.

## Model

### Consumer Search with Fatigue

A continuum of consumers search for products in a market with one or more firms. Unlike standard search models where search costs are fixed and constant, consumers in this model face **search fatigue**: the effective cost of continuing to search increases with the number of products already evaluated, or equivalently, the effectiveness of further search diminishes.

Formally, a consumer who has already searched $n$ products incurs a higher cost $c(n)$ for evaluating the $(n+1)$-th product, with $c'(n) > 0$. This increasing-cost structure captures the psychological and cognitive burden of processing information about multiple alternatives.

Consumers optimally decide at each stage whether to stop searching and purchase the best product found so far, or to continue searching at higher cost. As $n$ increases, the threshold quality required to justify continued search rises, making consumers more likely to stop early.

### Firm Strategies Under Search Fatigue

Firms anticipate consumers' search fatigue and adjust their strategies accordingly:

**Pricing:** Because fatigued consumers are less likely to continue searching after evaluating a product, firms can charge higher prices to consumers who encounter their product later in the search sequence. This creates **price dispersion** and **price cycles**: as the market evolves over time, prices vary systematically with the composition of searching vs. stopping consumers.

**Product proliferation:** A firm can reduce the effective per-product search cost faced by consumers by offering a broad product line. Consumers who visit a store with many products may find a satisfactory option within that store without having to search elsewhere. Product proliferation is thus a strategic response to search fatigue that keeps consumers within the firm's product space.

**Consumer assistance:** Firms may voluntarily provide consumer assistance services (product recommendations, simplified comparison tools, customer service) to reduce the search burden. By making product evaluation easier, firms attract consumers who have become too fatigued to search efficiently and are therefore more likely to purchase from the first helpful firm they encounter.

## Main Results

### Price Cycles and Time-Varying Prices

Search fatigue generates **cyclical price variation** that does not arise in standard search models. The mechanism is as follows:

- Fresh searchers (who have not yet evaluated many products) are less fatigued and more willing to continue searching if the price is too high. Firms must charge competitive prices to attract them.
- Fatigued searchers have accumulated high implicit costs of further search. Firms can extract more surplus from these consumers by charging higher prices, knowing they are unlikely to search further.

Over time, the distribution of consumers across fatigue levels evolves, generating systematic price cycles as firms respond to the changing composition of the market.

### Cross-Store vs. Within-Store Search

The effects of search fatigue differ depending on the locus of consumer search:

**Cross-store search (searching across firms):** Fatigue accumulates as consumers visit multiple stores. Firms benefit from being visited early in the search sequence (low-fatigue consumers) and benefit from having consumers stop early. This creates incentives for firms to be at the beginning of the search sequence and to make the first offer sufficiently attractive.

**Within-store search (searching within a single retailer's product line):** Fatigue accumulates as consumers browse through a store's product offerings. Firms benefit from product proliferation because consumers who become fatigued within the store are more likely to purchase something rather than leave empty-handed.

### Welfare Analysis

Consumer search fatigue reduces welfare relative to the frictionless benchmark because:

1. Consumers stop searching prematurely and purchase suboptimal products.
2. Firms use product proliferation and assistance services to reduce competition rather than to improve product quality.
3. Price cycles reduce consumer surplus by enabling systematic price discrimination based on fatigue levels.

The welfare loss from search fatigue is most pronounced when:
- Products are highly differentiated (so the cost of a suboptimal match is high)
- The fatigue rate is steep (consumers become tired quickly)
- There are many competing products (prolonging the search process)

Product proliferation and consumer assistance are privately profitable but may be socially wasteful if they primarily serve to redirect consumer attention within a firm rather than improving the quality of matches.

---

## Conclusion

When consumer search is not only costly but also tiring, standard search theory predictions break down. Markets with search fatigue exhibit price cycles, product proliferation, and voluntary consumer assistance — phenomena that cannot arise in models with constant search costs. These findings have implications for the design of financial product markets, retail markets, and any market where consumers must evaluate multiple complex alternatives before making a purchase decision. Policies that reduce search costs or require clearer product disclosures can mitigate the welfare losses from consumer search fatigue.
